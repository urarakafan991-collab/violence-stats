Sexual Orientation, Gender Identity, and Crime

Does sexual orientation or gender identity predict criminal behavior? Per-capita crime data for heterosexual men, heterosexual women, gay men, lesbian women, trans women, and trans men — compared against the same evidentiary standard. The UK prison infographic, the Dhejne study, the gay predator myth, and LGBTQ+ victimization data — all tested against primary sources.

✓ Live Last reviewed: Mar 2026 Published March 2026 20+ primary sources ~50 min read Methodology →
The data limitation

Almost no crime database in the U.S. or UK tracks sexual orientation or gender identity at the population level for offenders. The FBI, BJS, and UK MOJ record sex — not orientation or identity. What exists: prison snapshots (who is incarcerated on a given day), a handful of cohort studies, and victimization surveys. Anyone claiming definitive per-capita crime rates by sexual orientation is overstepping what the data allows. The strongest baseline in this literature is male vs. female, because that is what crime databases actually track.

Claim“Gay men are disproportionately pedophiles”
EvidenceNot supported. Jenny et al. (1994): of 269 child SA cases, only 2 perpetrators (0.74%) were identifiably gay/lesbian; 82% were heterosexual partners of close relatives. APA, CDC, and National Academy of Sciences all confirm no link. Paul Cameron’s contrary claims led to his expulsion from the APA (1983) and condemnation by three professional associations for data fabrication.
Claim“LGBTQ+ people are more violent/criminal overall”
EvidenceNot supported. The only population-level offender study (Netherlands, 2024) found men in same-sex relationships less likely to be suspected of crime (OR 0.685). LGB overrepresentation in U.S. prisons reflects criminalization history (sodomy laws, HIV statutes) and structural disadvantage, not innate propensity.
Claim“These concerns are overblown — LGBTQ+ people pose zero risk”
EvidenceOverstated. UK prison data is real even if misinterpreted. Documented cases of system-gaming exist (Karen White). Individual risk assessment in prisons is legitimate. The honest answer is that population-level data doesn’t exist to make definitive claims in either direction — but what exists does not support the predator narrative.

Part 1 of 7

The Comparison Framework

01 – Why This Matters Now

In 2025–2026, claims about LGBTQ+ people and crime directly drive federal and state policy. President Trump’s Executive Order 14168 (January 20, 2025) aimed to prevent “men [from] self-identify[ing] as women and gain[ing] access to intimate single-sex spaces.” The Heritage Foundation’s Project 2025 recommended that educators who “purvey transgender ideology” be registered as sex offenders. The ACLU tracked 339 anti-LGBTQ+ bills by February 10, 2025 alone, with over 1,022 anti-trans bills introduced across 49 states. At least 23 states had school bathroom bills by early 2026. Use of the term “groomer” directed at LGBTQ+ people surged 406% on social media following Florida’s “Don’t Say Gay” law (HRC/CCDH).

A per-capita comparison across all groups — including heterosexual cisgender baselines — is the only methodologically honest approach. Raw prison counts without population denominators prove nothing about relative risk.

The denominator choice for trans populations is the single most consequential variable in this entire debate. ONS itself downgraded the Census gender identity data in September 2024, noting question misinterpretation among non-English speakers. Using the ONS figure (~48,000 trans women) versus the GEO tentative estimate (~200,000+) changes per-capita rate calculations by an order of magnitude.

This is the dominant empirical reality against which all other groups must be measured. Men commit the overwhelming majority of sexual offenses across every available dataset:

Any claim about a minority group’s crime rate must be understood against this baseline: heterosexual cisgender men account for 93–98% of sexual offenses by every measure.

05 – Gay and Bisexual Men

No population-level per-capita sexual offense data exists for gay men specifically. The best evidence comes from the Netherlands:

Dutch total-population study (PMC 2024)Men in same-sex relationships LESS likely to be suspected of crime
Adjusted odds ratio0.685 (lower than heterosexual men)
Suspected at least once (1996–2020)14.1% same-sex vs. 22.4% opposite-sex

U.S. data shows LGB people overrepresented in prison (1,882 per 100,000 vs. 612 general rate), but this reflects criminalization history — sodomy laws (enforced until 2003), HIV statutes (felonies in 28 states with 20+ year sentences in 5 states), and solicitation enforcement — not elevated violent or sexual offending. The child abuse question is addressed in Part 4.

06 – Lesbian and Bisexual Women

No per-capita sexual offense data exists for this group. They are almost entirely absent from “predator” narratives, which is itself revealing: if the debate were about data, lesbian women would be analyzed with equal rigor. Their absence from the narrative confirms the discussion is about male-bodied people in female spaces, not about LGBTQ+ identity per se.

LBW are massively overrepresented in U.S. prisons (42.1% of women in state prison identify as sexual minority), but this reflects the “trauma-to-prison pipeline” — 53.7% of incarcerated sexual minority women experienced childhood sexual assault vs. 31.4% of straight women.

07 – Trans Women: The Core Controversy

This is the most politicized dataset. The headline numbers are real; their interpretation is where errors occur.

UK HMPPS 2017 (MOJ FOI)60 of 125 trans prisoners (48%) had sex offense convictions
UK HMPPS 2019 (FPFW FOI)76 of 129 (58.9%)
UK HMPPS 2024151 of 245 (61.6%)
Comparison: male prison pop16.8–22% sex offenders
Comparison: female prison pop3.3% sex offenders

These proportions are real and striking. But three factors fundamentally change how they should be interpreted. All three are addressed in Part 3.

08 – Trans Men

Trans men are almost completely absent from offender data. In Canada (CSC R-442), zero of 21 trans men in federal custody had sexual offense histories. In the UK, no trans men were found in the men’s prison estate. The March 2024 UK data showed “five or fewer” trans men with sexual offense convictions (suppressed for anonymity). This near-total absence is consistent with female-pattern offending baselines: people assigned female at birth offend at female rates regardless of gender identity.

The UK Prison Infographic: Why the Viral Claim Fails

09 – The Denominator Problem

Using the 2019 UK figure (76 trans women incarcerated for sex offenses), the per-capita rate changes dramatically by denominator:

ONS Census (48,000)158.3 per 100,000
Mid-range estimate (100,000)76.0 per 100,000
GEO low (200,000 UK, ~50% TW)76.0 per 100,000
GEO high (500,000 UK, ~50% TW)30.4 per 100,000
Cis male rate (comparison)~48 per 100,000
Cis female rate~4.1 per million (~0.41 per 100k)

The per-capita rate ranges from below the cis male rate (at higher denominators) to 3x above it (at the ONS figure) — a 40x uncertainty range. This is not a minor methodological caveat. The ONS figure of ~48,000 trans women is not a valid denominator for this calculation. ONS formally downgraded this data in September 2024, citing widespread question misinterpretation — particularly among non-English speakers — and explicitly stated the figure should not be used for per-capita analyses of this kind. Anyone citing “158 per 100,000” derived from the ONS Census figure is either unaware of ONS’s own disclaimer, or aware of it and choosing to ignore it. The statistic is not defensible on its own terms. Using denominators that ONS does not flag as unreliable, the per-capita rate for trans women falls at or below the cisgender male rate.

Source: UK MOJ FOI 2019 (76 sex offenders) • Multiple population estimates
10 – The Sentence-Length Snapshot Artifact

A prison population at any given moment is not a crime rate. It is an accumulation weighted by sentence length. In England and Wales, sexual offenses carry average custodial sentences of 66.2 months versus 20.3 months for all offenses — an accumulation factor of 3.3x. If a group has even a small number of sex offenders serving long sentences, a snapshot will show a high proportion of that group as sex offenders — even if their actual offending rate is identical to the general population.

The UK MOJ has explicitly noted that “prisoners on longer sentences are more likely to be managed as a transgender prisoner,” creating a filter effect that concentrates the most serious convictions in the counted population.

11 – The Canada 94% Finding

The Correctional Service Canada’s file review (Research Report R-442, 2022) examined 99 gender-diverse offenders in federal custody. Of 33 with sexual offense histories, 94% had committed their offenses before ever identifying as transgender — while still living as their birth sex. 82% of those with sex offense histories were trans women; 0% were trans men.

This finding is arguably the single most important piece of evidence in the entire debate. It means a prison snapshot that counts someone as a “trans woman sex offender” is overwhelmingly capturing people who offended as males before they ever claimed a transgender identity. The offending cannot be attributed to trans identity — it predates it.

12 – Dhejne et al. (2011): The Most Misquoted Study
MtF vs. cis female controls (any crime)HR 6.6 (CI 4.1–10.8) — significant
MtF vs. cis male controls (any crime)HR 0.8 (CI 0.5–1.2) — NOT significant
Pre-1989 cohort (any crime)Elevated vs. cis women — significant
Post-1989 cohort (any crime)HR 0.9 (0.6–1.5) — NOT significant

The study compared trans women to cisgender women, not cisgender men. Finding that trans women have higher crime rates than cis women (HR 6.6) means they have male-pattern crime rates — which is expected and does not show they offend more than cis men (HR 0.8, not significant). For the post-1989 cohort, no elevation was found at all. Dhejne herself stated: “We were certainly not saying that we found that trans women were a rape risk.”

13 – The HMP Downview Update

Since UK policy was strengthened in 2019 (now requiring that trans women with male genitalia or sex offense convictions cannot be held in the general women’s estate without ministerial exemption), there have been no sexual assaults committed by transgender women in women’s prisons. As of February 2026, 7 prisoners were on E Wing at HMP Downview, the separate unit for trans women.

The Gay Predator Myth: Historical Receipts

14 – Anita Bryant and “Save Our Children” (1977)

When Dade County, Florida passed an anti-gay discrimination ordinance, singer Anita Bryant formed “Save Our Children, Inc.” and claimed: “Homosexuals cannot biologically reproduce children; therefore, they must recruit our children.” The campaign gathered 64,000+ signatures, voters repealed the ordinance 69% to 31%, and Florida barred gay adoption for 20 years. Two weeks after the vote, Robert Hillsborough was stabbed 15 times in San Francisco while his attackers chanted “Faggot.” The 2025–2026 “groomer” rhetoric uses an identical structure.

16 – What the Science Actually Shows
Jenny et al. (1994, Pediatrics)269 child SA cases: only 2 perpetrators (0.74%) identifiably gay/lesbian
Heterosexual partners of relatives222 of 269 cases (82.5%)
Groth & Birnbaum (1978)Zero exclusively homosexual men among 175 convicted child sex offenders

Groth’s distinction is critical: pedophilia is attraction to prepubescent children. It is clinically distinct from adult sexual orientation. Most child molesters of boys do not identify as homosexual in their adult orientation — their attraction is to age, not gender.

19 – Trans Homicides and Sexual Assault

HRC has tracked 399 transgender or gender-nonconforming people killed in the U.S. since 2013. Of cumulative victims, 73.7% were Black transgender women and 84.4% were BIPOC. The 2015 U.S. Transgender Survey (n=27,715) found 47% of respondents had been sexually assaulted in their lifetime — compared to ~20–25% for the general female population (CDC NISVS). Trans people in prison are 10x as likely to be sexually assaulted by fellow inmates.

Steelman: The Case for Elevated Risk

20 – The Strongest Data-Informed Case
UK prison data is real50–62% of trans prisoners have sex offense convictions. Even under the most generous denominator (~250,000 trans women), the per-capita rate (~30 per 100k) remains within the same order of magnitude as cis men (~48 per 100k) — not trivially small.
System-gaming cases existKaren White — a repeat sex offender with no surgery, no hormones, no GRC — was placed in a women’s prison and sexually assaulted two female inmates. Isla Bryson (Scotland) similarly offended. These are not just anecdotes; they demonstrate real vulnerabilities.
Dhejne pre-1989 cohortThe pre-1989 cohort did show significantly elevated crime vs. cis women (HR 6.6 for any crime, 18x for violent crime). While the post-1989 cohort did not reach significance, the study had limited statistical power.
Precautionary principleWhen data is uncertain and stakes involve vulnerable populations (incarcerated women, children), policy can reasonably err on the side of caution through individual risk assessment.
Male socializationPeople raised as male may retain male-pattern offending regardless of later gender identity. Dhejne’s finding that trans women’s crime rate was indistinguishable from cis men’s (HR 0.8) is consistent with this.
Individual risk assessmentEven if group-level data shows no elevation, individual risk assessment for prison housing — considering offense history, risk factors, and current anatomy — is legitimate and prudent.

Steelman: The Case That the Claims Are Wrong

21 – The Strongest Rebuttal
The baseline dominatesHeterosexual cisgender men commit 93–98% of sexual offenses by every measure. Any policy response should be proportionate to where the actual risk lies.
No population-level elevationThe only population-level offender study (Dutch, 2024) found gay men less likely to offend. Dhejne post-1989 showed no significant elevation. Canada: 94% of trans-identified offenders committed their crimes before ever identifying as transgender.
Prison snapshots ≠ crime ratesThe 3.3x sentence-length accumulation factor mechanically inflates sex offender proportions. The UK MOJ itself notes trans prisoners are filtered for long sentences.
Gay predator myth: dead on arrivalJenny (2/269 = 0.74%), Groth (zero exclusively gay among 175), APA, CDC, NAS — the scientific consensus is unambiguous and has been for 30 years. Cameron was expelled and condemned by four organizations.
Victims 5x more than perpetratorsLGBTQ+ people are 5x more likely to be violently victimized (NCVS), 9x more likely to face hate crimes, and 47% of trans people report lifetime sexual assault. The ratio of victimization to perpetration is astronomically skewed.
Historical patternEvery generation picks a marginalized group to label as predators: Black men in the early 20th century, gay men from the 1970s through 2000s, trans women today. The claims follow the same structure, use the same flawed methodologies, and are promoted by the same networks. Each was eventually debunked.

Summary — Claims vs. Evidence
Claim“Trans women commit sexual offenses at male rates or higher”
EvidenceNot supported when properly denominated. UK prison snapshot data shows high proportions of trans prisoners with sex offenses (58–62%), but these are prison headcounts, not crime rates. The per-capita rate varies by a factor of 40x depending on denominator choice. Canada’s Correctional Service found 94% of sex offending by trans-identified prisoners occurred before the individual identified as transgender — while still living as their birth sex. Dhejne (2011) found no significant difference between trans women and cis men.

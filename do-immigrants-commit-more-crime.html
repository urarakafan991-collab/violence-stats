Immigration and Crime: What the Data Actually Shows

Do immigrants commit more crime than native-born Americans? Incarceration rates, Texas arrest data, 150 years of Census records, ecological studies, the European evidence, tested against primary sources.

✓ Live Last reviewed: Mar 2026 Published March 2026 15+ primary sources ~35 min read Methodology →
The data problem

Most U.S. crime databases do not record immigration status. Texas is effectively the only state with a large arrest dataset that systematically links arrestees to DHS status information — which is why it dominates this debate. The distinction between legal and undocumented immigrants matters throughout: they face different screening, different incentive structures, and different data availability. Area-level correlations and individual-level incarceration data measure different things and should not be confused with each other.

Claim“Texas DPS data proves illegal aliens commit hundreds of thousands of crimes”
EvidenceThe raw counts are real — 597,000+ offenses charged since 2011 — but Texas DPS itself says these figures do not show undocumented immigrants commit more crime than other groups. They are counts, not rates. Per-capita analysis of the same Texas data shows undocumented immigrants have the lowest felony arrest rates.
Claim“Immigrants are less crime-prone than natives, full stop”
EvidenceDirectionally supported in U.S. per-capita data across decades, but overstated as a universal claim. European asylum-seeker contexts sometimes show different patterns. Legal immigrants in Texas had higher sexual-assault arrest rates than natives. Data gaps outside Texas create genuine uncertainty about national generalizability.
Claim“Sanctuary cities make communities less safe”
EvidenceNot supported by ecological studies, which find sanctuary policies associated with similar or lower crime rates. However, specific cases of declined ICE detainers followed by reoffending are real, even if they do not alter the population-level picture.

Part 1 of 7

The Claim and Why It Matters

01 – The Political Context

In 2025–2026, the claim that immigrants — especially undocumented immigrants — commit crime at higher rates than native-born Americans has become central to federal policy. The Trump administration’s January 20, 2025 executive order “Protecting the American People Against Invasion” framed immigration enforcement as a public-safety priority. The Laken Riley Act, signed January 29, 2025, mandated ICE detention for noncitizens charged with theft, burglary, or similar crimes. ICE arrests doubled post-inauguration, with DHS reporting 70% of arrests involved people convicted of or charged with crimes.

The opposing claim — that immigrants are less crime-prone than the native-born — is backed by most of the modern U.S. empirical literature. But public perception remains divided: Pew found in February 2024 that 57% of Americans said the migrant influx leads to more crime, while PRRI found in October 2024 that 43% agreed immigrants increase local crime and 54% disagreed.

02 – Why the Legal vs. Undocumented Split Matters

This distinction is not a technicality — it is central to interpreting every dataset in this article. Legal immigrants undergo background checks and admissibility screening that may select against criminality. Undocumented immigrants face deportation risk as an additional deterrent but lack systematic national tracking in most crime databases. Political rhetoric often blurs the two, citing total “criminal alien” removals that include both statuses, or raw counts that do not disaggregate precisely.

Data availability favors the split: ACS/Census residual methods can identify legal vs. undocumented populations; Texas DPS uses DHS Secure Communities/IDENT matching for arrests. Outside Texas, almost no jurisdiction systematically records status at arrest or conviction.

Part 2 of 7

What the Data Actually Shows

03 – Incarceration Rates

The most recent national estimate comes from Cato Institute analysis of 2023 ACS data (Landgrave & Nowrasteh, Policy Analysis No. 994, April 2025). Using a modified residual method for ages 18–54:

Native-born1,221 per 100,000
Undocumented613 per 100,000
Legal immigrants319 per 100,000
GapUndocumented exactly half as likely; legal 74% less likely

The gap is not new. Butcher & Piehl (NBER Working Paper 13229) tracked men ages 18–40 across Census decades: native-born institutionalization was 1.35% vs. immigrants 0.42% in 1980, widening to 3.45% vs. 0.68% by 2000 — sample sizes exceeding 1.8 million native-born and 350,000 immigrants.

Abramitzky et al. (NBER/AER 2024) extended the series from 1870 to 2020, finding immigrants have had lower incarceration rates than the U.S.-born for most of the modern period since 1960 — approximately 60% less likely, even after controlling for age, education, race, and state.

Source: Cato ACS 2023 (ages 18–54) • Butcher & Piehl Census data (men 18–40)
04 – Texas Arrest Rates: The Best Status-Specific Data

Texas is uniquely informative because its DPS criminal history system links arrestees to DHS status data with 96% compliance. Light et al. (PNAS 2020) analyzed all jailable felony arrests 2012–2018:

Violent felony (native)213.0 per 100k
Violent felony (undocumented)96.2 per 100k
Property (native)165.2 per 100k
Property (undocumented)38.5 per 100k
Drug (native)337.2 per 100k
Drug (undocumented)136.0 per 100k
Homicide (native)4.8 per 100k
Homicide (undocumented)1.9 per 100k

Undocumented immigrants had the lowest felony arrest rates across every category shown. Legal immigrants were usually in the middle, with two exceptions: sexual assault (legal: 31.2, native: 18.2) and homicide (legal: 5.0, native: 4.8) — where legal immigrants were at or above native rates in this dataset.

Sensitivity analysis: to make undocumented violent-crime rates reach parity with natives, the actual undocumented population in Texas would have to be less than 45% of the accepted estimate. For property crime, 23%. These thresholds are implausibly low.

Source: Light et al. (PNAS 2020) • Texas DPS/DHS linked felony arrests 2012–2018
05 – The Raw Counts vs. Rate Distinction

Texas DPS also publishes running raw counts that are the most politically cited numbers in this debate. As of March 2026: 335,000+ people classified as illegal noncitizens charged with 597,000+ offenses, including 1,123 homicide charges and 7,629 sexual assault charges, with 224,000+ convictions since June 2011.

These numbers are real. But Texas DPS itself states these figures do not establish that undocumented immigrants commit more crime than other groups. They are counts without population denominators. When researchers apply denominators to the same underlying Texas data, the per-capita rates consistently show lower rates for undocumented immigrants.

06 – Area-Level Studies

The broad U.S. ecological literature finds no positive association and often a negative one between immigration and local crime. The National Academies issue brief (2015) found that higher immigrant concentrations are associated with lower crime. The Dallas Fed review concluded the empirical evidence “comes down decidedly” on the side of immigrants being less likely to commit crime. The Ousey & Kubrin meta-analysis found the average association was negative but weak, with 62% of effect sizes statistically insignificant.

The ecological-fallacy caveat matters: a county with more immigrants and less crime does not automatically prove each immigrant is individually less crime-prone. That is why the strongest analysis uses individual-level data (incarceration and arrest) alongside area-level data, not in place of it.

Part 3 of 7

The Immigrant Paradox and International Context

07 – The Generational Convergence

First-generation immigrants show markedly lower crime rates than comparable native-born people, but the advantage shrinks across generations. 2000 Census data: foreign-born Mexican men 0.70% incarcerated vs. U.S.-born Mexican 5.90%; Salvadoran/Guatemalan foreign-born 0.52% vs. U.S.-born 3.01%; Vietnamese foreign-born 0.46% vs. U.S.-born 5.60%.

Leading explanations: positive self-selection (migration costs/risks filter for law-abiding traits), deportation deterrence for noncitizens, stronger family/community controls in immigrant communities, and then gradual convergence through acculturation — the second generation adopts native behavioral patterns including higher-risk ones.

08 – The Age-Crime Curve Problem

Immigrants — especially undocumented immigrants — are disproportionately young working-age males, the peak offending demographic. This composition should elevate their aggregate crime rates. Yet they still show lower rates than natives. This makes the lower-crime finding more impressive, not less: the gap persists or widens after age/sex adjustment in every major study.

09 – European Data: A Different Picture

European patterns differ in ways that matter. Germany (BKA 2024): temporary migrants (asylum seekers, undocumented) were 8.8% of criminal suspects vs. ~4.3% of population. Sweden: foreign-background individuals ~2.5x overrepresented among violent-crime suspects. Greece: a 1-percentage-point increase in refugee share increased crime incidents 1.7–2.5 percentage points.

The U.S.–Europe gap is not one side lying. The contexts genuinely differ: U.S. immigration combines family, labor, and screened refugee migration; Europe’s recent debates center on asylum seekers in legal limbo with weak work access and mass proximate inflows. Italy’s legalization evidence (Pinotti, AER 2017) is instructive: legalization reduced serious crime from 2.9 to 1.2 per 100 applicants, strongly suggesting that irregular status and blocked work access matter.

Part 4 of 7

Steelman: The Case That Immigration Raises Crime

10 – The Strongest Data-Informed Restrictionist Case

A serious restrictionist argument is not “all immigrants are criminals.” It is this:

Data gaps Most jurisdictions do not track status nationally. Texas dominates the debate because almost no one else can separate legal from undocumented at scale. This creates genuine uncertainty about generalizing Texas to the whole country.
Offense-specific exceptions Even in Texas, legal immigrants had higher sexual-assault arrest rates (31.2) than natives (18.2) and roughly equal homicide rates. Subgroup and offense-type patterns matter more than the headline average.
European evidence Germany, Sweden, and Greece show that some migrant subgroups — especially asylum seekers with poor work access — can raise crime in certain settings. The U.S. is not Europe, but conditions can change.
Absolute vs. per-capita “Immigrants commit less crime per capita” is not the same as “immigration adds zero crimes.” Any large population with a nonzero offending rate produces victims in absolute terms. Texas DPS raw counts — 1,123 homicide charges, 7,629 sexual assault charges — represent real people.
The policy-failure argument Even a low per-capita rate may be unacceptable to voters if the offender could have been screened out, denied entry, detained, or removed earlier. This is the logic behind the Laken Riley Act: any preventable victimization from a removable person is a policy failure.
Recidivism and reentry Repeat immigration offending is real. In FY2024, 72.4% of federal immigration cases involved illegal reentry. Deported criminals who return add uncounted offenses. The 18-month reapprehension rate for male Mexican border crossers was 24.2% before sanctions reduced it.
Part 5 of 7

Steelman: The Case That Immigrants Commit Less Crime

11 – The Strongest Empirical Case
Weight of evidence The peer-reviewed literature is remarkably consistent: Butcher & Piehl (1980–2000 Censuses), Rumbaut (2000 Census subgroups), Light et al. (Texas arrests), Abramitzky et al. (150-year series), and Cato ACS analyses all point in the same direction. Immigrants — legal and undocumented — have substantially lower incarceration and arrest rates.
Self-selection Migration is costly, risky, and effortful. People who successfully migrate may be more future-oriented, more attached to work and family, and less willing to risk everything through crime. Dallas Fed identifies positive selection and stronger family ties as leading explanations.
Deterrence effect A U.S.-born citizen risks jail; an undocumented immigrant risks jail and deportation with a permanent bar. This creates a uniquely powerful additional deterrent that the Texas research explicitly notes as consistent with findings.
Historical consistency Every wave of immigration — Irish, Italian, Chinese, Mexican, Central American — triggered identical moral panics about crime. The data repeatedly showed the same thing: lower rates. The pattern has now been documented across 150 years of Census data.
Perception gap 57% of Americans (Pew 2024) believed the migrant influx leads to more crime. The per-capita evidence does not support this. Media coverage amplifies rare immigrant-committed crimes (availability heuristic) while native crime — the vast majority — is normalized.

Who Says What: The Debate’s Key Voices

12 – Restrictionist Voices
Tom Homan White House Border Czar; former Acting ICE Director. 34-year law enforcement career. Cites 650,000 noncitizens with criminal histories on the ICE Non-Detained Docket. Critique: Figure is cumulative over decades, includes non-violent offenses and pending charges; per-capita analyses show lower rates.
Jessica Vaughan Director of Policy Studies, Center for Immigration Studies (CIS; classified as a hate group by SPLC). Frequent congressional witness. Cites ICE detainer data showing 22,000+ criminal aliens released by sanctuary jails since 2021. Critique: Raw detainer counts lack per-capita adjustment; sanctuary-city ecological studies show no crime increase.
Lora Ries Director, Heritage Foundation Border Security Center. JD, nearly 30 years in DHS/immigration work. Argues border insecurity and lax enforcement lead to preventable crime; pairs victim cases with enforcement mechanics. Critique: Strongest on enforcement logic, weaker on the population-level per-capita question.
Texas DPS State law enforcement agency. Publishes running criminal noncitizen data (597,000+ offenses since 2011). Most politically cited dataset by restrictionists. Critical note: Texas DPS itself says these figures do not show undocumented immigrants commit more crime. They are counts, not rates.
13 – Pro-Immigration Voices
Alex Nowrasteh Senior VP for Policy, Cato Institute. BA George Mason, MS LSE. Author of Cato ACS incarceration analyses. Key finding: undocumented incarceration rate exactly half that of natives (613 vs. 1,221 per 100k, 2023). Critique: ACS residual method may undercount some undocumented prisoners; Cato is libertarian/pro-immigration.
Michael T. Light Professor of Sociology, University of Wisconsin–Madison. PhD Penn State. NIJ-funded researcher. Published the definitive Texas arrest-rate study (PNAS 2020): undocumented violent-felony rate 96.2 vs. native 213.0 per 100k. Critique: Texas-specific, may not generalize nationally.
Ran Abramitzky Professor of Economics, Stanford; NBER. With Boustan et al., constructed a 150-year immigrant incarceration series (1870–2020). Finding: immigrants 60% less likely to be incarcerated than U.S.-born men since 1960. Critique: Historical long-run data may not capture very recent post-2021 arrivals.
LAPD / PERF Chief Jim McDonnell (LAPD) and Chuck Wexler (Police Executive Research Forum). Law enforcement voices arguing immigrant trust is essential for policing and that this population is “not generally driving crime trends.” Critique: Restrictionists say this prioritizes community relations over cooperation with federal enforcement.
Part 7 of 7

Data Limitations and Confidence Ratings

14 – What the Data Does NOT Answer Well

National undocumented offending outside Texas; fine-grained offense categories across states; refugee-specific U.S. crime effects; recidivism after deportation for non-immigration crimes; whether local police behavior changes arrest rates differently by status. A true national linked dataset of arrests, convictions, immigration status, and exposure time by state would matter most. It does not exist.

Summary — Claims vs. Evidence
Claim“Immigrants are committing a crime wave”
EvidenceNot supported by per-capita data. As the immigrant share of the U.S. population grew from 6.2% (1980) to 13.9% (2022), the total crime rate dropped ~60%. Individual-level incarceration and arrest data consistently show immigrants at lower rates than natives.

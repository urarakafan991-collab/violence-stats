The Mpemba Effect — Does Hot Water Freeze Faster Than Cold?

It sounds impossible. A Tanzanian student noticed it in 1963 making ice cream. Aristotle recorded it in 350 BCE. Sixty years of modern physics research later, the answer is still contested — and the reasons why are more interesting than the question.

Part 1 of 6

What Is Actually Being Asked

A precise question with an imprecise history

“Does hot water freeze faster than cold water?” is too vague to be a scientific question. To answer it honestly, you need to specify at minimum: the starting temperatures, water composition, container shape and material, cooling environment, and — most critically — what you mean by “freeze.”

Does “freeze” mean the first ice crystal appears? The surface reaches 0°C? The center thermocouple reads 0°C? The entire volume is solid? These are not the same event. They can happen in different orders in the same apparatus. Changing the definition can reverse the conclusion of the same experiment. This definitional ambiguity is not a side issue — it is the central reason this debate has persisted for over two thousand years.

The scientifically precise question is: Under what specific, reproducible conditions does a body of water with a higher initial temperature reach a defined freezing endpoint sooner than an otherwise identical body starting at a lower temperature? The evidence says this question cannot be answered with a flat yes or no. It is answered: “Yes, under some conditions, no under others — and the physics is still not fully understood.”

Part 2 of 6

The History: From Aristotle to a Tanzanian Student

2,300 years of observation

Aristotle wrote in Meteorologica (Book I, Chapter XII, c.350 BCE): “The fact that the water has previously been warmed contributes to its freezing quickly: for so it cools sooner. Hence many people, when they want to cool water quickly, begin by putting it in the sun.” He attributed it to antiperistasis — the supposed intensification of a quality by its opposite. The explanation is wrong; the observation is plainly stated.

Francis Bacon wrote in Novum Organum (1620): “Slightly tepid water freezes more easily than that which is utterly cold.” René Descartes noted in his Discourse on Method (1637) that water kept long on a fire freezes faster, attributing it to evaporation of particles “least able to resist bending.”

Erasto Mpemba and Denis Osborne

In 1963, Erasto Mpemba was a Form 3 student at Magamba Secondary School in Tanzania making ice cream with classmates. Facing a shortage of refrigerator space and running late, he poured his hot boiled milk mixture directly into the freezer while classmates waited for theirs to cool. His hot mixture froze solid in about 90 minutes. The cooled mixture remained liquid. His physics teacher dismissed the result as impossible. Classmates joked about “Mpemba’s physics.”

Years later at Mkwawa High School, physicist Denis Osborne visited for a lecture. Mpemba asked publicly: “If you take two similar containers with equal volumes of water, one at 35°C and the other at 100°C, and put them in a refrigerator, the one that started at 100°C freezes first. Why?” Osborne, to his credit, did not dismiss it. He told Mpemba he did not know but would test it. Back in Dar es Salaam, Osborne’s technician confirmed the observation.

They published their findings jointly in Physics Education in 1969 under the title “Cool?” — reporting that in their specific setup (70 ml samples in 100 ml beakers on polystyrene in a domestic freezer), time to onset of freezing was shortest for water starting near 90–100°C. The paper also noted that evaporation accounted for at most ~30% of the cooling, pointing toward other mechanisms.

The scientific and human importance of the Mpemba story

Osborne’s response to Mpemba is a model for scientific practice: a student observes something that contradicts a teacher’s expectations, persists despite ridicule, and is eventually taken seriously by a physicist willing to test rather than dismiss. The observation was correct. The phenomenon is real under the conditions they tested. That a 15-year-old Tanzanian student’s ice cream observation launched 60 years of physics research is one of the better stories in modern science.

In 2012 the Royal Society of Chemistry offered £1,000 for the best explanation of the effect, receiving over 22,000 submissions. The winner, Nikola Bregović of the University of Zagreb, proposed a combination of supercooling and convection. Subsequent commentary noted the prize was won but the question was not closed.

Part 3 of 6

Why It Should Be Impossible — and Why It Sometimes Isn’t

The thermodynamic argument against it

Newton’s Law of Cooling states that the rate of heat loss from an object is proportional to the temperature difference between the object and its surroundings: dQ/dt ∝ ΔT. A hotter sample has a larger initial ΔT and loses heat faster at first — but it has more total heat to lose before reaching 0°C. Naïve integration of the cooling equation predicts the colder sample must always reach 0°C first. This is not a rounding error or approximation. It is straightforward energy accounting.

Any claim that hot water freezes first appears to violate this. The way out, as Burridge and Linden (2016) note, is that it is not thermodynamically impossible in principle — only in the naive model. If heating changes the system in any relevant way (mass via evaporation, dissolved gas content, convection pattern, nucleation behavior, thermal contact), then the hot sample is no longer just the cold sample plus extra heat. It has become a different physical system. The race is between two non-identical samples, and the faster cooler can sometimes lose.

The proposed mechanisms
Proposed Physical Mechanisms — Evidence Summary Six decades of research
EvaporationReal effect; hot water loses mass, less to freeze. But accounts for ~30% of cooling in original experiment; covered-container studies still show effect. Partial explanation only.
Convection currentsStrong support. Hot water develops stronger natural convection (higher Rayleigh number), transporting heat to the surface more efficiently. RSC winner Bregović and Vynnycky-Maeno models both support this as major contributor.
Supercooling differencesStrong experimental support (Auerbach 1995; Brownridge). Cold water is more prone to supercooling — remaining liquid below 0°C until nucleation. If cold water supercools to −6°C while hot freezes at 0°C, hot water enters its phase change first.
Dissolved gasesPlausible. Hot water holds less dissolved air; degassing alters nucleation sites. Mpemba-Osborne tried to control for this; later studies show mixed results. Likely contributes in some water types.
Hydrogen bond restructuringControversial. Tao et al. (2017, SMU/Nanjing) propose heating alters hydrogen bond network in ways that accelerate cooling. Not consensus; requires further experimental confirmation.
Frost layer / thermal contactReal in ordinary freezers: hot container melts frost on shelf, improving thermal contact. Cold container insulated by frost layer. Mpemba-Osborne controlled for this using polystyrene blocks.

Crucially, these mechanisms are not mutually exclusive. In any given experiment, several may operate simultaneously in the same direction — or some may cancel others. This is why results are condition-sensitive and why no single mechanism has been accepted as the complete explanation.

Part 4 of 6

Steelmanning Both Sides

The strongest case that the effect is real

The original Mpemba-Osborne experiment was not anecdotal — it was a systematic set of measurements published in a peer-reviewed journal. Brownridge (2011, arXiv) reported observing hotter water freezing first in 28 of 28 trials under his specific conditions, with the key variable being supercooling differences. Burridge and Hallstadius (2020) were able to make the effect reliably reproducible by deliberately increasing nucleation sites for the warmer sample — providing evidence of the physical mechanism even if engineered.

A 2023 InfoMat paper reported direct observation of the Mpemba Effect with water under specific conditions. The 2026 RSC Applied Interfaces study (Janni et al.) conducted 176 measurements and observed the effect in 46–58 instances depending on definition, concluding that convective air current fluctuations and supercooling variation were responsible. The effect is real in these setups.

Additionally, Mpemba-like effects have been observed in other systems entirely: tetrahydrofuran clathrate hydrates, granular fluids, magnetic minerals, and even quantum systems. This suggests the underlying phenomenon — a system in a higher-energy initial state relaxing faster under certain conditions — is not specific to water but reflects broader non-equilibrium thermodynamics.

The strongest case that the effect is not real (or not meaningful)

Burridge and Linden (University of Cambridge, Scientific Reports, 2016) conducted careful controlled experiments defining freezing as cooling to 0°C and found no consistent evidence for the effect. Their analysis showed that some published positive results were explainable by subtle measurement artifacts — including a vertical mismatch of as little as 1 cm in thermometer position, which could falsely produce an apparent Mpemba Effect because the water column is not isothermal.

Their broader critique is powerful: when “freezing” is defined as cooling to 0°C and the experiment is tightly controlled, the naïve prediction holds. Positive results often appear when multiple variables change simultaneously with temperature — meaning the effect may be real in those setups but not attributable to temperature alone.

“Real” overclaim
“Hot water always freezes faster” — false. No universal law. The effect appears in specific setups and vanishes in others. Presenting it as a reliable fact without specifying conditions is misleading.
“Myth” overclaim
“It violates thermodynamics, therefore impossible” — false. The naive thermodynamic argument assumes identical systems. Heating produces a different physical system. Dismissing the effect without engaging with the mechanism arguments misses the actual physics debate.
Part 5 of 6

What the Evidence Actually Shows

The honest verdict

The Mpemba Effect is real but highly condition-dependent. It is not a universal law. It is not a myth. The evidence supports this precise middle ground:

The effect is most reliably observed with: starting temperature differentials around 30–50°C; open containers where evaporation contributes; tap water (with dissolved minerals) rather than deionized water; domestic freezer temperatures just below freezing (−5°C to −10°C); and measurement of onset of freezing (first ice formation) rather than complete solidification.

The effect disappears or is undetectable with: tightly identical container placement; covered containers preventing evaporation; deionized, degassed water; measurement defined as cooling to 0°C at the center; and carefully controlled nucleation sites.

No single mechanism explains all observations. The weight of evidence points to convection and supercooling differences as the dominant contributors in most setups where the effect appears. Evaporation contributes in open systems. The hydrogen bond hypothesis is interesting but not yet confirmed experimentally at the macroscopic level.

What the 2026 study found

Janni, Botero Ampudia, and Dahlberg (RSC Applied Interfaces, 2026) conducted 176 measurements of water freezing under various conditions. They observed Mpemba-like effects in 46 instances by one definition and 58 by another. Their conclusion: the effect arises from fluctuations in convective air currents and variations in supercooling among water samples — not from any intrinsic property that reliably produces the effect regardless of conditions. Real, but stochastic and condition-dependent.

What we do not know

After 60 years of modern research and centuries of prior observation, physics still lacks a single accepted theory explaining all Mpemba Effect observations. We understand the candidate mechanisms. We do not have a predictive model that specifies exactly which conditions will produce the effect and by how much. The precise role of hydrogen bond restructuring remains unconfirmed. The relationship between container geometry, nucleation site distribution, and the probability of observing the effect is still being mapped.

This is not a failure of science — it is an honest representation of where a genuinely hard problem stands after sustained investigation.

Part 6 of 6

What This Tells Us

What this debate tells us about science

The Mpemba Effect demonstrates how science handles counterintuitive claims. The correct skeptical response to Mpemba’s 1963 observation was not “impossible, thermodynamics forbids it.” It was: define the claim precisely, isolate variables, and see if the result survives cleaner experiments. Osborne did this. The result survived some tests and not others. That is where the science has been ever since.

The debate also illustrates the difficulty of reproducibility when phenomena are highly condition-sensitive. Two labs testing the “same” question but with different container materials, water sources, freezer temperatures, and freezing definitions are not actually testing the same thing. Their contradictory results are both valid within their own parameters. This is not a crisis — it is what science looks like when the phenomenon is subtle and multi-causal.

Factors most coverage misses

The definition problem is central, not peripheral. Most popular coverage presents the Mpemba Effect as a yes/no question. The actual answer is: yes under definition A and conditions X, no under definition B and conditions Y. Any article that does not specify what “freezing” means is not answering the question.

Mpemba-like effects exist beyond water. The same anomalous relaxation pattern has been observed in clathrate hydrates, granular fluids, and quantum systems. The physics is broader than a kitchen experiment about ice cream.

Practical implications exist but are narrow. Ice cream makers do use warmed mixtures. Industrial freezing processes and ice rink resurfacing involve temperature considerations. But “hot water sometimes freezes faster in specific setups” does not translate into universal kitchen advice.

The hydrogen bond hypothesis matters even if unconfirmed. If heating genuinely restructures water’s hydrogen bond network in ways that persist during cooling and affect nucleation, it would represent a new physical mechanism with implications beyond ice formation. The question is still live in molecular physics.

Common claims vs. what the evidence shows
Claim“Hot water always freezes faster than cold — it’s a proven law”
EvidenceFalse. No universal law exists. The effect is real but highly condition-dependent — it appears under specific temperature ranges, container types, and water purities, and disappears under controlled conditions in several rigorous replications.
Falsifiability threshold — what would change this conclusion

This article concludes the effect is real but condition-dependent with no single accepted mechanism. This would change if:

1. A large pre-registered multi-lab study demonstrated the effect consistently across standardized conditions with a clear, agreed definition of freezing — which would upgrade it from “condition-dependent” to “robust.”

2. Spectroscopic evidence confirmed that hydrogen bond restructuring during heating persists into the cooling phase and demonstrably affects nucleation kinetics at the macroscopic level.

3. Multiple independent labs reproduced the Burridge-Linden null result under every proposed positive-result condition — which would support the “experimental artifact” interpretation.

Critical Prefatory Note

This article synthesizes data from multiple independent databases that measure different things using different methodologies. FBI hate crime data uses voluntary law enforcement reports. The New America Foundation tracks terrorism charges and incident records. ADL counts extremist-related murders. ACLED codes protest events. None of these are interchangeable. Cross-category comparisons are made carefully and caveated throughout. Any report — political or journalistic — that merges these systems as if they were one dataset is overstating certainty.

Claim“Muslim Americans are a terrorism-prone population”
EvidenceNot supported. Kurzman's longitudinal research shows Muslim Americans have among the lowest per-capita terrorism involvement of any religious group. Total US fatalities from Muslim-American violent extremism since 9/11 represent <0.04% of all US murders in the same period.
Claim“Far-left and far-right violence are comparably lethal”
EvidenceNot supported. New America finds 1 post-9/11 death from far-left terrorism vs. 137 from far-right. CSIS data: 13 left-wing fatalities in the past decade vs. 112 right-wing. Left-wing property destruction incidents are higher; left-wing lethality is dramatically lower.
Claim“LGBT people pose an elevated risk to children”
EvidenceNot supported. APA, CDC, and peer-reviewed criminological research find no evidence that sexual orientation or gender identity predicts risk of child sexual abuse perpetration. Studies find the overwhelming majority of perpetrators are heterosexual individuals with family or institutional access.
Claim“Anti-Christian hate crimes are a major crisis”
EvidenceNot supported at the scale claimed. OSCE/FBI data (2024): 279 anti-Christian offenses vs. 2,137 anti-Semitic and 288 anti-Muslim. Anti-Christian political discourse frequently conflates policy disputes and social criticism with physical violence — these are distinct and must be separated.

Definitions: Why They Matter More Than You Think

01 – The Structural Problem

Every number in this article depends on definitions — and the most consequential fact about the American data landscape is that the major databases are not measuring the same thing. An attack like the 2022 Buffalo supermarket shooting appears in hate crime statistics, terrorism statistics, and extremist murder statistics — but as a different entry in each system, coded by different rules, and producing different aggregate totals depending on which you consult. Using numbers from these systems interchangeably without disclosure is the single most common error in public discourse on this topic.

02 – The Motive Standard (Applied Consistently)

This report enforces a single standard across all ideological categories: identity or affiliation is not sufficient to attribute an act of violence to an ideological cause. Only documented motive does. This means:

A Muslim individual who commits violence without documented Islamist ideological motivation is not classified as Islamist terrorism here — the same way a Christian individual who commits violence without documented Christian nationalist motivation is not classified as Christian terrorism. A white perpetrator is not classified as white nationalist terrorism without documented ideology. A gay or trans person who commits violence is not classified as LGBT-motivated violence without documented motive.

This is the only methodologically defensible standard. It is applied symmetrically to every category in this report — including categories where applying it may reduce the apparent scale of violence attributed to ideologies that are politically convenient targets.

The 9/11 Problem

The September 11 attacks killed 2,977 people in a single day — more than all documented far-right domestic terrorism fatalities over any 24-year period combined. Including 9/11 in a time-series comparison with other domestic terrorism categories makes 2001 so large that all other years become invisible, and makes every subsequent year look artificially safer. This report treats 9/11 as a separate data point in every analysis, exactly as every serious terrorism researcher does. Failure to do this is the most common manipulation in public discourse on terrorism fatalities.

04 – Trend Analysis

The ADL trend data shows far-right extremist murders declining from a peak of 79 in 2017 to 13 in 2024 — but the organization notes this decline reflects fewer mass-casualty events, not a reduction in the underlying movement. The DHS Homeland Threat Assessment of October 2020 concluded that racially and ethnically motivated violent extremists — specifically white supremacist extremists — would remain the most persistent and lethal threat in the homeland.

Meanwhile, CSIS data shows that while left-wing incidents surpassed right-wing incidents in frequency for the first time in 30+ years by early 2025, the lethality of far-right violence remains substantially higher. Incident counts and fatality counts are different metrics — and politically, each side tends to use whichever one supports their preferred narrative.

The Pulse Nightclub Problem

The 2016 Orlando Pulse nightclub shooting (49 killed) is listed in some databases as far-right/anti-LGBT and in others as Islamist. The cleanest answer: it is both, and neither purely. Omar Mateen pledged allegiance to ISIS during the attack (Islamist), but investigators found he had previously visited the venue, had documented personal conflicts, and may have selected a gay bar from several possible targets. FBI materials classify it as both a terrorist attack and a hate crime. This report does not use Pulse as a clean data point for either the far-right or Islamist category.

06 – Steelman: Conservative Critiques of the Category
Argument “Far-right” is stretched too broadly. Critics including scholars at Claremont and Heritage argue the category sweeps in mentally ill lone actors, online-radicalized individuals with incoherent ideologies, and ordinary criminals who happen to have right-wing social media activity — none of whom represent a coherent organized movement. Conflating The Base/Atomwaffen (organized terror networks) with isolated individuals inflates apparent scale.
Assessment This objection has partial merit. ADL, CSIS, and New America all apply stricter standards requiring documented ideological content before attribution. But edge cases remain genuinely contested — some incidents coded as far-right by one database are coded as mass violence or personal grievance by another. The directional finding (far-right is the most lethal recent domestic category) survives these disputes, but precise counts are sensitive to coding decisions.
Argument FBI surveillance bias inflates the right-wing count. Post-9/11, FBI/DHS deployed substantially more surveillance resources against right-wing extremist groups. More surveillance of a group produces more documented plots, arrests, and attributions. Kurzman documented that DOJ press releases named right-wing terrorism defendants inconsistently while naming Muslim-American defendants routinely — creating asymmetric data capture.
Assessment A legitimate methodological point. It does not reverse the directional finding — multiple independent systems converge on far-right dominance in lethality — but it may affect the precise ratio between categories.

09 – The Property vs. Lethality Distinction

This is among the most politically manipulated distinctions in the domestic violence debate. The ELF/ALF eco-terrorism wave of the 1990s–2000s produced hundreds of arsons targeting property — at one point the FBI declared ELF the top domestic terrorist threat — with zero directly attributed deaths. The 2020 protest period produced significant property destruction alongside rare homicides. Collapsing arson of an empty car dealership and murder into the same terrorism category is a definitional error with major political valence; this report does not do it.

Progressive Far-left violence is overcounted by including property destruction as terrorism. The ELF explicitly maintained a “no one gets hurt” policy; targeting empty buildings is categorically different from targeting people. Historically, COINTELPRO targeted left-wing groups with surveillance and disruption, creating a legacy of over-documentation of left-wing activity.
Conservative Left-wing violence during 2020 was systematically underprosecuted, with prosecutors in Democratic-run cities declining charges against arrested protesters, artificially deflating documented counts. The “93% peaceful” figure is mathematically true but operationally misleading: 5% of 10,600+ events is 530+ violent incidents causing billions in damage concentrated in specific neighborhoods. Those residents were harmed.
Assessment Both positions have legitimate empirical support. Both are used to manipulate the overall picture. The honest synthesis: most protests were peaceful; some violence was real and caused significant harm; far-left violence causes dramatically fewer deaths than far-right; property destruction should be reported but not conflated with homicide.

The global context is essential and frequently omitted: The overwhelming majority of Islamist terrorism victims globally are Muslim. The GTD and Fondapol's global dataset find that approximately 88–89% of deaths from Islamist terrorism occur in Muslim-majority countries — Iraq, Afghanistan, Pakistan, Nigeria, Somalia, Syria. ISIS and al-Qaeda have killed far more Sunni and Shia Muslims than non-Muslims by absolute count. Omitting this fact from discussions of Islamist terrorism is not neutral — it actively distorts the picture of who bears the burden of this violence.

Civil liberties Entrapment and surveillance bias inflate Muslim terrorism statistics. ACLU and Kurzman document that the proportion of foiled-to-executed plots is dramatically higher for Islamist cases than for right-wing cases. Some “plots” in the count involve FBI informants playing central roles in facilitating them. More surveillance produces more documented plots, regardless of underlying threat level.
National security Islamist terrorism poses a structurally different mass-casualty risk. The per-capita frequency argument does not address the tail risk of a coordinated mass-casualty attack like 9/11. Al-Qaeda and ISIS have demonstrated operational planning for attacks of that scale — something domestic lone-actor right-wing violence has not replicated. Both threats are real and both deserve resources.

13 – The Underreporting Problem

Every figure in this section is a lower bound. FBI hate crime data depends on voluntary reporting by ~15,000 law enforcement agencies. Many submit zero reports — not because zero hate crimes occur in their jurisdictions, but because they lack classification protocols, training, or will. The Bureau of Justice Statistics' National Crime Victimization Survey, which captures self-reported victimization, consistently finds 2–3 times more hate crime victimizations than the UCR — approximately 243,000–247,000 per year in the 2005–2019 period versus roughly 7,000–11,000 in UCR annual reports.

14 – Anti-Black Hate Crimes

Anti-Black incidents make up over 51% of all race-based hate crimes — consistently the largest single racial category across multiple years of FBI data. Black Americans comprise approximately 13.7% of the US population; anti-Black victimization is plainly not proportional to population share.

Historical context is essential: the Equal Justice Initiative has documented more than 4,400 racial terror lynchings of African Americans between 1877 and 1950 — framing them explicitly as terrorism against the Black population. Church burnings targeting Black congregations have occurred in documented waves, including over 100 fires in 1995–1996. The 2022 Buffalo shooting and 2015 Charleston shooting represent the lethal end of contemporary white supremacist violence targeting Black Americans.

15 – Anti-Semitic Hate Crimes

Anti-Jewish hate crime is consistently the largest single religious hate crime category in the US — a finding that has held across every year of FBI data collection since 1991. In 2023, anti-Jewish incidents reached their highest recorded level: 1,832 FBI-reported incidents, a 63% increase from 2022, driven in part by the October 7 Hamas attacks and their aftermath.

ADL's 2024 Audit of Antisemitic Incidents recorded 9,354 incidents — the highest in its 46-year series. The ADL figure and the FBI figure measure different things: ADL includes non-criminal harassment and vandalism that never gets reported to police. They must not be compared directly.

Perpetrators of antisemitic attacks span multiple ideological categories: far-right (Pittsburgh Tree of Life, Poway synagogue), Islamist-adjacent (Jersey City 2019), and post-October 7 protest-related harassment. The debate about whether criticism of Israeli government policy constitutes antisemitism is genuine and contested — this report notes it as a definitional dispute that materially affects aggregate counts without resolving it politically.

Anti-LGBT Violence & Related Violence

18 – Anti-LGBT Hate Crimes (Overview)

In 2024, sexual orientation motivated 17.2% of all reported hate crimes — 2,008 incidents. Gender identity motivated an additional 3.9% — 455 incidents. Combined, nearly one in five reported hate crimes targeted LGBTQ+ individuals.

These figures are among the most severely undercounted in all hate crime categories. LGBTQ+ victims systematically underreport to police due to: fear of discrimination by officers, fear of being outed, distrust of law enforcement based on historical targeting of LGBTQ+ communities, and in some cases, fear that identifying their identity to law enforcement will itself create harm.

19 – Anti-Transgender Violence: The Per-Capita Debate

The Human Rights Campaign Foundation has tracked at least 372 transgender and gender-expansive homicide victims since 2013, including approximately 32–44 per year in recent years. This tracking relies on media reports, community monitoring, and advocacy networks — it captures deaths that law enforcement may misclassify. In 2021, HRC documented at least 57 trans homicides while federal health data recorded 2 — a discrepancy driven primarily by misgendering of victims in official records, not actual differences in incidence.

The Per-Capita Complexity

The per-capita homicide rate comparison for transgender people is genuinely complex. A 2017 study in the American Journal of Public Health (Dinno) found that overall trans homicide rates may be comparable to or even lower than cisgender rates when controlling for demographic exposure factors — because trans people are less frequently involved in contexts (drug trade, gang activity) that drive most homicide. However, young Black trans women face dramatically elevated homicide rates — an estimated 1 in 2,600 chance, compared with 1 in 12,000 for the young cisgender population. These two findings coexist: the overall rate is contested; the Black trans women subgroup disparity is one of the most robust findings in the data.

21 – Violence Involving LGBT Perpetrators: What the Research Shows

This section exists for intellectual completeness and TruthBased.org's steelmanning standard.

The claim that LGBTQ+ individuals pose elevated risk to children is made frequently in political discourse. The peer-reviewed evidence does not support it. Studies by Groth and Birnbaum (1978), Jenny et al. (1994), and subsequent reviews consistently find that the overwhelming majority of child sexual abuse is committed by heterosexual individuals with family or institutional access. The APA, the American Academy of Pediatrics, and major child protection organizations find no scientific basis for the claim that LGBTQ+ identity is a risk factor for child sexual abuse perpetration. Studies that have claimed otherwise have been methodologically criticized for conflating pedophilia (a paraphilia) with sexual orientation, or for using non-representative samples. HIGH confidence for the refutation of this claim.

On general violent crime: research comparing LGBT and non-LGBT perpetration rates is sparse because sexual orientation is inconsistently recorded in criminal justice data. The available evidence does not support a claim of elevated violent crime perpetration rates among LGBTQ+ individuals. LGBTQ+ individuals are significantly more likely to be victims than perpetrators across all available data.

22 – Conversion Therapy as Documented Harm

Major medical and mental health organizations — APA, AMA, WHO, American Academy of Pediatrics — have concluded that conversion therapy causes harm and does not achieve its stated goals. Studies document elevated rates of PTSD, depression, and suicidality among survivors. The Trevor Project's 2022 National Survey found LGBTQ+ youth who underwent conversion therapy were more than twice as likely to attempt suicide. 22+ US states have enacted bans on conversion therapy for minors. This constitutes documented, severe institutional harm against the LGBTQ+ population, included here because the publication covers systemic harms alongside physical violence.

Conservative Controlling for police encounter rates reduces the disparity. The argument: Black Americans have higher rates of police encounters due to higher rates of violent crime in some communities; adjusting for encounter rate narrows or eliminates the racial shooting disparity in some models (e.g., Fryer 2016). The disparity may reflect structural exposure rather than officer-level bias.
Assessment The National Academies has warned that using arrest or encounter rates as denominators can embed earlier-stage bias, making adjusted disparities appear smaller than the underlying problem. A 2020 reanalysis by Knox and Mummolo found strong evidence of anti-Black disparities in killings of unarmed Americans even after controlling for these factors. Fryer's specific dataset and methodology have been disputed. Current scholarly consensus: the raw disparity is well-established (HIGH confidence); the causal decomposition between direct officer bias and structural exposure remains contested (MEDIUM confidence).
Progressive The denominator itself is biased. Controlling for arrest rates cannot remove bias if the arrest rate is itself racially biased — you cannot use a biased measure to control for bias. The disparity must be understood as a product of systems with bias at multiple stages, not solely as an artifact of individual officer decisions.

25 – Underreporting Hierarchy (Most to Least)

Not all hate crime categories are undercounted equally. The mechanisms differ: victim distrust of police, inability to report, definitional exclusion, and jurisdictional fragmentation each create distinct patterns. The following ranking is a judgment call, not a settled metric:

  1. Disability-based hate crimes — victims often cannot report; institutional abuse categorized separately; law enforcement rarely identifies the bias motive
  2. Anti-transgender hate crimes — victims fear outing and discrimination; most trans homicides are misgendered in official records, removing them from the trans-violence count entirely
  3. Anti-Indigenous/Native American violence — jurisdictional complexity on reservations; inadequate federal reporting infrastructure; chronic underfunding of tribal law enforcement
  4. Violence against undocumented migrants — victims fear deportation; lack legal standing in many circumstances; geographic isolation in border regions
  5. Anti-LGBT (non-trans) — fear of outing; many victims don’t know their experience qualifies as a hate crime
  6. Anti-Black, anti-Hispanic, anti-Asian — significant underreporting, though better documented than the categories above
  7. Anti-Jewish hate crimes — relatively better-reported due to ADL infrastructure and community reporting networks; still undercounted
  8. Anti-Christian hate crimes — possibly over-reported relative to severity; political discourse inflates the perceived scale
26 – What the Full Body of Evidence Supports
HIGH CONFIDENCE
Far-right/anti-government violence has been the deadliest domestic extremist category in the post-9/11 era by multiple independent measures. This finding is directionally robust even accounting for legitimate methodological disputes about category definitions.
HIGH CONFIDENCE
Muslim Americans have among the lowest per-capita terrorism involvement of any religious community. Islamist violent extremism accounts for <0.04% of all US murders in the post-9/11 period. This does not eliminate the mass-casualty threat posed by transnational Islamist networks — both facts can be true simultaneously.
HIGH CONFIDENCE
Anti-Jewish hate crimes are consistently the largest religious hate crime category in the US. Anti-Black hate crimes are consistently the largest racial category. Both findings are stable across years of FBI data.
HIGH CONFIDENCE
LGBTQ+ individuals face violent victimization at 5x the rate of non-LGBTQ+ people (NCVS). Black trans women face especially elevated homicide risk — approximately 1 in 2,600 vs. 1 in 12,000 for the young general population. Official FBI hate crime counts severely undercount LGBT victimization.
HIGH CONFIDENCE
LGBTQ+ identity does not predict risk of child sexual abuse perpetration. This claim has no credible empirical foundation in peer-reviewed research and is definitively contradicted by APA, CDC, and major criminological bodies.
HIGH CONFIDENCE
The racial disparity in police killings is large and consistent: Black Americans are killed at approximately 2.8x the per-capita rate of white Americans. The causal decomposition between direct officer bias and structural exposure is an active scholarly debate; the disparity itself is not.
27 – What the Evidence Does NOT Support
Summary — Claims vs. Evidence
Claim“Far-right terrorism is the biggest domestic threat”
EvidenceDirectionally supported post-9/11: far-right killed ~137 vs. ~121 from Islamist attacks (New America). But 9/11 itself — a singular outlier — killed 2,977. The category comparison requires disclosing which time window and which database you are using.
Claims the data does not support

• That left-wing political violence is comparably lethal to right-wing domestic terrorism. Incident counts are rising; fatality counts are dramatically lower — conflating these metrics is deliberate manipulation.

• That Muslim Americans are disproportionately responsible for terrorism. The per-capita rate is among the lowest of any religious community. Kurzman’s research is peer-reviewed and methodologically rigorous.

• That anti-Christian hate crimes represent a crisis comparable in scale to anti-Jewish or anti-Muslim hate crimes. The numbers are documented and consistently lower.

• That LGBTQ+ individuals pose elevated risk to children. No credible peer-reviewed evidence supports this; major institutions explicitly contradict it.

• That 9/11 represents an ongoing pattern of Muslim-American terrorism. It was a singular mass-casualty event executed by foreign nationals; it belongs in its own analysis, not in time-series comparisons with domestic terrorism trends.

• That the 2020 BLM protests were a violent insurrection. ACLED data shows 95%+ of events were peaceful. Significant violence and property destruction did occur in a minority of events; neither the “insurrection” framing nor the “entirely peaceful” framing is accurate.

• That all political violence is basically one-sided. The data shows different magnitudes by metric — fatalities favor right-wing; incident counts show left rising; hate crime victimization falls heaviest on groups that are political targets of right-wing rhetoric. This pattern is complex and should be reported as such.

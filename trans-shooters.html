Trans People & Mass Shootings

Why this is filed under Political Fact Checks

This claim — that trans people are overrepresented as mass shooters — has been actively used by elected officials and political organizations to justify legislation targeting the trans community. Rep. Ronny Jackson called for removing trans people from "the streets and the internet." The Heritage Foundation petitioned the FBI to designate "Transgender Ideology-Inspired Violent Extremism" as a domestic terror threat. The Trump DOJ suggested revoking trans people's right to possess firearms. These are political claims, driven by political actors, aimed at a specific community. The data is examined here as a political fact check, not a demographic study.

TL;DR — The headline numbers

Zero of three major databases show trans people overrepresented as mass shooters. Under the broadest definition (GVA): ~5× underrepresentation per capita. Under the strictest (Mother Jones/VPP): 1 confirmed trans shooter out of 149–201 incidents. In 2020–2025 specifically: 2 out of 3,516 incidents. The claim does not survive contact with the data under any consistent methodology. Nine of ten sensitivity analysis combinations produce underrepresentation.

Ethical note. Categorizing mass shooters by any identity group — race, gender, religion, or transgender status — carries inherent risks of stigmatization. We perform this analysis solely because the claim is being used to justify legislation targeting a specific community. We rely only on public law enforcement records and court filings for identity classification. Where sources conflict, cases are labeled “disputed” and excluded from confirmed counts.

The Denominator Problem

An intentional methodological choice — explained

Parts 1 and 2 used 1.43 million as the trans population denominator — binary trans adults only, deliberately excluding nonbinary people. This was a conscious choice to use the most conservative possible figure. Even with that undercount, trans people remain underrepresented in every database. The August 2025 Williams Institute report places the full trans adult population at 2.1 million. When that figure is used, underrepresentation increases substantially.

Part 4

Limitations, Counterarguments & Sensitivity Analysis

13 — The Small-Sample Problem
Critical statistical limitation

Every per-capita figure in this analysis is calculated from 1 to 5 confirmed events spread across a population of 1.4–2.1 million people and thousands of total mass shootings. The 95% confidence interval on the underlying all-time GVA rate is roughly 0.03% to 0.20%. The direction (underrepresentation) is clear across every calculation; the exact magnitude has wide error bars and should not be treated as a precise figure. A single newly verified case would shift the all-time rate from 0.09% to 0.10% — the qualitative conclusion would not change, but the magnitude would.

14 — The Strongest Counterargument: Crime Prevention Research Center / Lott
Crime Prevention Research Center (CPRC) / John Lott
Primary source of the overrepresentation claim · actively cited in policy debates
OPPOSING CLAIM
The Claim
Lott and CPRC argue that transgender individuals are overrepresented among mass shooters by 4–7× when examining public indiscriminate mass shootings between approximately 2016–2023. Their published list includes 8–10 individuals identified as trans or nonbinary.
Their Method
Uses a strict "public indiscriminate" definition, counts 8–10 trans/nonbinary perpetrators, and divides by a trans population share of approximately 0.3–0.5% — an older estimate from earlier CDC surveys.
Issue 1
The population estimate of 0.3–0.5% is outdated. The Williams Institute's August 2025 report places the adult trans population at 0.8% (1.43M binary, 2.1M including nonbinary). Using the current figure cuts or eliminates the apparent overrepresentation.
Issue 2
Lott's list includes Butler (never confirmed by law enforcement), Aldrich (post-arrest legal claim only), and Van Rootselaar (Canadian, not in any US database). Removing unverified cases brings his count to 4–5.
Issue 3
Even accepting his full list of 8–10 and his population estimate of 0.5%: 8 shooters / 700,000 trans adults × 100,000 = 1.14 per 100k. The GVA general population rate = 1.76 per 100k. This still shows underrepresentation. The comparison uses the GVA baseline deliberately: if we are asking whether trans people are more dangerous to the general public than anyone else, the appropriate baseline is the rate at which anyone commits a mass shooting — which is the GVA rate. You cannot apply a strict counting filter only to trans shooters and not to the general population; that produces a biased numerator against an unfiltered denominator.
Issue 4
Overrepresentation in Lott's framing only appears when using share-of-incidents (not per-capita) combined with an outdated, lower population estimate. These are not equivalent measures.
What Remains
Lott's strongest legitimate point: if you use share-of-incidents rather than per-capita, and use an outdated low population estimate, and count all disputed/unconfirmed cases, a surface-level overrepresentation can be constructed. All three methodological choices must be applied simultaneously — and all three favor the overrepresentation conclusion in ways that are not individually defensible.
2026 Update
Lott's 2026 posts add Dorgan (Pawtucket) to his list. Dorgan was a targeted domestic shooting — excluded under every strict definition Lott claims to prefer. Including domestic incidents while citing a "public indiscriminate" definition is internally inconsistent.

16 — What This Data Cannot Establish
Age & Sex Confounders
Mass shooters are overwhelmingly young males (~97–98% natal male). The trans population skews younger. Age-standardized and sex-stratified rates would be more precise but require data not currently available in public databases.
Mental Health Overlap
Trans adults have higher rates of depression and anxiety, partly attributed to minority stress. Mass shooters also have high rates of documented mental illness. This overlap would need to be controlled for in a rigorous analysis. This site does not make causal claims about trans identity and violence.
Classification Replicability
The identity verdicts here are based on sourced reporting. Readers are encouraged to check the primary sources linked for each shooter profile to independently verify.
Causation vs. Correlation
Even if rates were higher, this data would not establish trans identity as a causal factor. Individual-level factors — mental illness, social isolation, notoriety-seeking, access to weapons — are documented in each confirmed case. Trans identity is not cited as a motive by investigators in any case on this site.

Conclusion — What The Evidence Shows

0 of 3
Zero of the three major databases show trans people overrepresented as perpetrators. The broadest definition (GVA) shows ~9× underrepresentation by share of incidents (~5× per capita). The strictest (VPP/Mother Jones) produces exactly one confirmed trans shooter out of 149–201 incidents.
2/3,516
In the exact five-year window most cited in policy debates (2020–2025), two confirmed trans people committed GVA mass shootings out of 3,516 total incidents. Trans people were ~14× underrepresented by share of incidents, and ~7.5× less likely per capita than the general population.
0
Zero confirmed trans shooters on this site had a motive attributed to their transgender identity by law enforcement. Confirmed motives: notoriety-seeking, mental illness, domestic violence, retaliation for bullying — none caused by or unique to being trans.
9/10
Nine out of ten assumption combinations in the sensitivity analysis produce underrepresentation. The single combination producing overrepresentation requires three simultaneous methodological choices, none of which are individually defensible.
4×
Trans people are approximately four times more likely to be victims of violent crime than perpetrators of it. The evidence more accurately reflects the opposite of the political narrative being advanced. Source: GLAAD / Human Rights Campaign 2025
Bottom line

Every database. Every time window.
Every population figure. Every assumption.
The result is the same: underrepresentation.

The claim that transgender people are disproportionately responsible for mass shootings does not survive contact with the data under any methodologically consistent calculation. Trans people are statistically less likely — not more likely — to commit mass shootings than the general population. Political actions taken against the trans community on the basis of this claim are not supported by the evidence.

Falsifiability threshold

This conclusion would change if independent researchers produced verified documentation of additional confirmed transgender mass shooters sufficient to bring the per-capita rate above the baseline population rate. Under the strictest definition (4+ killed, public): approximately 3–5 additional confirmed cases in the 2013–2026 window would move the rate to proportional representation. If such documentation is produced, this article will be updated. The dataset and classification criteria are transparent and reproducible.

Mental health context. Research shows many mass shooters had documented suicidality or grievance narratives, but population-level psychiatric diagnoses are poor predictors of mass violence. We record only explicit, documented indicators from court records and medical examiner statements — not speculative diagnoses. Being transgender is not a mental illness, and this analysis does not treat it as one.
Context: Trans people as victims of violence. While this article examines the claim that trans people are overrepresented as perpetrators, the data on victimization runs in the opposite direction. FBI hate crime statistics show anti-transgender bias incidents have risen every year since tracking began in 2013, reaching a record high in 2022 (the most recent complete year). The Human Rights Campaign documented at least 320+ violent deaths of transgender and gender non-conforming people in the U.S. between 2013 and 2025, disproportionately Black trans women. Trans people are approximately four times more likely to be victims of violent crime than perpetrators of it. The political narrative frames trans people as threats; the data frames them as targets.
Frequently Asked Questions
Q: Is this saying transgender people don’t commit crimes?
No. This analysis concerns a narrow class of mass shooting events and does not generalize to other crimes or contexts. It addresses only the specific political claim about mass shooting overrepresentation.
Q: Could cases be hidden or misclassified?
We model this explicitly in the sensitivity analysis (Part 4). Even under the most aggressive inclusion assumptions — counting all disputed, misidentified, and ambiguous cases — nine of ten assumption combinations still produce underrepresentation.
Q: Does this prove causation?
No. These counts are descriptive, not causal. This article does not claim that being transgender prevents mass shootings. It establishes that the political claim of overrepresentation is not supported by the data.
Q: Why does this article exist at all?
Because the claim is being used to justify legislation — including proposals to revoke firearms rights and designate trans people as domestic terror threats. Claims used to restrict civil rights must meet a high evidentiary bar. This one does not.
